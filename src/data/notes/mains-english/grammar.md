# English Grammar — MPSC Group-C Combined 2026 (Mains English)

## 1. The 12 Tenses — Overview
- English tense system = 3 main times (Present, Past, Future) × 4 aspects (Simple, Continuous/Progressive, Perfect, Perfect Continuous) = 12 tenses.
- Tense is shown by the form of the verb; aspect shows whether an action is simple, ongoing, completed, or ongoing-till-a-point.
- Auxiliary verbs used: am/is/are, was/were, has/have/had, will/shall, be + being, have/has/had + been.

## 2. Present Tense — Forms, Structure, Examples
- **Simple Present**: Structure: Subject + V1 (+s/es for 3rd person singular). Use: habitual actions, universal truths, fixed schedules. Example: "She writes a letter every day." / "The sun rises in the east." Negative: Subject + do/does + not + V1. Question: Do/Does + Subject + V1?
- **Present Continuous**: Structure: Subject + am/is/are + V1+ing. Use: action happening now, temporary action, near-future planned action. Example: "She is writing a letter now." / "I am meeting him tomorrow."
- **Present Perfect**: Structure: Subject + has/have + V3 (past participle). Use: action completed in near past with present relevance, action started in past continuing to present (with "since/for"). Example: "She has written the letter." / "I have lived here since 2015."
- **Present Perfect Continuous**: Structure: Subject + has/have + been + V1+ing. Use: action started in past and still continuing, emphasizing duration. Example: "She has been writing the letter for two hours." Signal words: since, for, how long.

## 3. Past Tense — Forms, Structure, Examples
- **Simple Past**: Structure: Subject + V2 (past form). Use: completed action at a definite time in past. Example: "She wrote a letter yesterday." Negative: Subject + did not + V1. Question: Did + Subject + V1?
- **Past Continuous**: Structure: Subject + was/were + V1+ing. Use: ongoing action in the past, often interrupted by another action. Example: "She was writing a letter when he came." "was/were" agree with subject number.
- **Past Perfect**: Structure: Subject + had + V3. Use: action completed before another past action ("earlier of two past actions"). Example: "She had written the letter before he arrived." Often used with "before," "after," "by the time."
- **Past Perfect Continuous**: Structure: Subject + had + been + V1+ing. Use: action continuing up to a certain point in the past, emphasizing duration before another past event. Example: "She had been writing the letter for two hours before he came."

## 4. Future Tense — Forms, Structure, Examples
- **Simple Future**: Structure: Subject + will/shall + V1. Use: prediction, spontaneous decision, promise. Example: "She will write a letter tomorrow." (Traditionally "shall" with I/We, "will" with others — modern usage mostly "will" for all.)
- **Future Continuous**: Structure: Subject + will/shall + be + V1+ing. Use: action that will be ongoing at a specific future time. Example: "She will be writing the letter at 5 pm tomorrow."
- **Future Perfect**: Structure: Subject + will/shall + have + V3. Use: action that will be completed before a specific future time. Example: "She will have written the letter by 5 pm."
- **Future Perfect Continuous**: Structure: Subject + will/shall + have + been + V1+ing. Use: action continuing up to a specific point in the future, emphasizing duration. Example: "By next year, she will have been working here for ten years."

## 5. Active Voice and Passive Voice
- **Active Voice**: Subject performs the action. Structure: Subject + Verb + Object. Example: "The teacher teaches the students."
- **Passive Voice**: Object of active sentence becomes subject; action is received. Structure: Object + be (am/is/are/was/were/been/being) + V3 + by + Subject. Example: "The students are taught by the teacher."
- **Rule of conversion**: Identify tense of active sentence → keep the same tense in passive using correct form of "be" → change main verb to V3 (past participle) → object becomes subject, subject becomes "by + agent" (may be dropped if unimportant/unknown).
- **Tense-wise passive forms**:
  - Simple Present: is/am/are + V3 (e.g., "The letter is written.")
  - Present Continuous: is/am/are + being + V3 (e.g., "The letter is being written.")
  - Present Perfect: has/have + been + V3 (e.g., "The letter has been written.")
  - Simple Past: was/were + V3 (e.g., "The letter was written.")
  - Past Continuous: was/were + being + V3 (e.g., "The letter was being written.")
  - Past Perfect: had + been + V3 (e.g., "The letter had been written.")
  - Simple Future: will/shall + be + V3 (e.g., "The letter will be written.")
  - Future Perfect: will/shall + have + been + V3 (e.g., "The letter will have been written.")
  - Modals (can/could/may/might/must/should): Modal + be + V3 (e.g., "The work can be done.")
- **Note**: Present Perfect Continuous, Past Perfect Continuous, Future Continuous, and Future Perfect Continuous are rarely/never converted to passive (awkward/no standard passive form).
- **Special rules**: Imperative sentences → passive uses "Let" (e.g., "Open the door" → "Let the door be opened."); Interrogative sentences (Wh-questions) → passive keeps question word and inverts auxiliary (e.g., "Who wrote this letter?" → "By whom was this letter written?"); Yes/No questions → auxiliary comes first (e.g., "Does she write the letter?" → "Is the letter written by her?").
- **Verbs with two objects** (give, teach, offer, send): can form two passives (e.g., "He gave me a book" → "I was given a book by him." / "A book was given to me by him.")
- **Intransitive verbs have no passive form** (e.g., "He sleeps" cannot be converted to passive since there is no object).

## 6. Direct and Indirect Speech (Narration)
- **Direct Speech**: Exact words of the speaker are quoted within inverted commas. Example: He said, "I am going to school."
- **Indirect (Reported) Speech**: Speaker's words reported without quotation marks, often with changed tense/pronouns. Example: He said that he was going to school.
- **General Rules for Conversion**:
  - Remove inverted commas; use conjunction "that" (optional in informal, compulsory in formal/exam usage) for statements.
  - Change pronouns according to person: 1st person pronoun changes according to subject of reporting verb; 2nd person changes according to object of reporting verb; 3rd person remains unchanged.
  - If reporting verb is in Past Tense, tense of reported speech generally shifts one step back (backshift):
    - Simple Present → Simple Past
    - Present Continuous → Past Continuous
    - Present Perfect → Past Perfect
    - Simple Past → Past Perfect
    - Past Continuous → Past Perfect Continuous
    - Present Perfect Continuous → Past Perfect Continuous
    - Will/shall → would; Can → could; May → might; Must → had to/must
  - No backshift needed if reporting verb is in Present/Future tense, or if reported speech states a universal truth/habitual fact (e.g., "He said that the earth revolves around the sun.")
- **Change of Time and Place References**:
  - now → then; today → that day; tomorrow → the next day/the following day; yesterday → the day before/previous day; here → there; this → that; these → those; ago → before; last night → the previous night; next week → the following week.
- **Assertive (Statement) Sentences**: Reporting verb "said to" → "told"; use conjunction "that." Example: She said, "I am happy." → She said that she was happy.
- **Interrogative (Question) Sentences**: Reporting verb changes to "asked/enquired/wanted to know"; remove question mark; word order becomes statement order (Subject + Verb).
  - Yes/No questions: use "if" or "whether." Example: He said, "Do you like tea?" → He asked if/whether I liked tea.
  - Wh-questions: retain the question word, no "if/whether," no "that." Example: She said, "Where do you live?" → She asked where I lived.
- **Imperative Sentences** (orders/requests/advice): Reporting verb changes according to tone — "ordered/commanded" (for commands), "requested" (for requests), "advised" (for advice), "forbade" (for negative commands); infinitive "to" is used before the verb; "please" is dropped or replaced by "requested."
  - Example: He said, "Go away." → He ordered him to go away.
  - Example: She said, "Please help me." → She requested him to help her.
  - Example: The teacher said, "Do not talk." → The teacher forbade them to talk / told them not to talk.
- **Exclamatory Sentences**: Reporting verb changes to "exclaimed with joy/sorrow/wonder," etc.; interjections (Oh!, Alas!, Hurrah!) are removed and appropriate phrase added.
  - Example: He said, "Hurrah! We have won." → He exclaimed with joy that they had won.
  - Example: She said, "Alas! I have failed." → She exclaimed with sorrow that she had failed.
- **Optative Sentences** (wishes): Reporting verb changes to "wished/prayed."
  - Example: He said, "May you live long!" → He wished that I might live long.

## 7. Articles (a, an, the)
- English has two types: **Indefinite articles** (a, an) and **Definite article** (the).
- **"A"** is used before words beginning with a consonant sound (e.g., a book, a university — "u" sounds like "yu"), and before words beginning with a consonant letter.
- **"An"** is used before words beginning with a vowel sound (a, e, i, o, u sound) (e.g., an apple, an hour — "h" is silent, an MBA — "M" sounds like "em").
- **Indefinite articles (a/an)** used for: singular countable nouns mentioned for the first time; general/non-specific reference; expressing rate/ratio (e.g., "Rs. 100 a kilo"); with certain numbers (a hundred, a dozen, a thousand).
- **Definite article "the"** used for: specific/particular nouns already mentioned or known; superlative degree (the best, the tallest); unique objects (the sun, the moon, the earth, the sky); rivers, seas, oceans, mountain ranges (the Ganga, the Arabian Sea, the Himalayas); names of newspapers (The Times of India); nationalities as a whole (the Indians); musical instruments (play the guitar); ordinal numbers (the first, the second); before superlatives and "only" (the only book); historical periods/events (the Mughal period, the Freedom Struggle); countries with plural/collective names (the United States, the Philippines, the UK).
- **Zero article (no article)** used before: proper nouns (names of people, cities, most countries — India, Mumbai); abstract nouns used in general sense (Honesty is the best policy); material nouns in general sense (Gold is precious); plural nouns used in general sense (Dogs are loyal); names of languages (She speaks Marathi); names of meals unless specified (We have breakfast at 8); single mountains/lakes (Mount Everest, Lake Victoria).
- **Common MPSC trap examples**: "an hour," "an heir," "an honest man" (silent h → an); "a university," "a European," "a one-rupee note," "a united team" (consonant sound → a).

## 8. Prepositions
- Preposition = word placed before a noun/pronoun to show its relation to another word in the sentence (place, time, direction, manner, agent, etc.).
- **Prepositions of Time**: at (point of time — at 5 o'clock, at night, at noon), on (days/dates — on Monday, on 15th August), in (months/years/seasons/long periods — in July, in 2026, in the morning), since (starting point of time — since 2020), for (duration — for two hours), by (before a deadline — by 5 pm), during (throughout a period — during the exam).
- **Prepositions of Place**: at (specific point — at the door), in (enclosed space — in the room, in Mumbai), on (surface — on the table), under/below (beneath), over/above (higher than), between (two things), among (more than two), beside/next to (near), in front of, behind.
- **Prepositions of Direction/Movement**: to (destination), into (entering), onto (moving to a surface), from (origin), through (passing within), across (from one side to other), towards, up, down, along.
- **Common Fixed Prepositional Usage (frequently tested)**: good at, bad at, afraid of, fond of, proud of, ashamed of, tired of, capable of, aware of, angry with (a person), angry at (a situation), married to, similar to, different from, depend on, insist on, congratulate on, arrive at (small place), arrive in (large place/country), interested in, believe in, succeed in, prevent from, deal with, agree to (a proposal), agree with (a person), listen to, look at, look after (take care), look for (search), look into (investigate), apply for, apologize for, consist of, comprised of, according to, in spite of, on behalf of, in accordance with, on the basis of.
- **Preposition placement**: In formal English, preposition is placed before its object; in informal English (especially in questions), it may appear at the end (e.g., "What are you looking for?").
- **No preposition before**: home (go home, not "go to home" when "home" means one's own house), "discuss" (discuss the matter, not "discuss about"), "reach" (reach the station, not "reach to"), "enter" (enter the room, not "enter into the room" — though "enter into an agreement" is correct idiomatically).

## 9. Subject-Verb Agreement (Concord)
- **Basic Rule**: A singular subject takes a singular verb; a plural subject takes a plural verb.
- **Rule 1**: Two subjects joined by "and" usually take a plural verb (e.g., "Ram and Shyam are friends.") — Exception: if the two nouns refer to one single idea/person, verb is singular ("Bread and butter is my breakfast." / "The Secretary and Treasurer is present" — one person).
- **Rule 2**: Subjects joined by "or," "nor," "either...or," "neither...nor" take a verb agreeing with the nearer subject (e.g., "Either he or his friends are coming." / "Neither the teacher nor the students were present.")
- **Rule 3**: Collective nouns (team, family, jury, government, committee, crowd, army) take a singular verb when acting as one unit, and plural verb when members act individually (e.g., "The team is playing well." / "The team are quarrelling among themselves.")
- **Rule 4**: Indefinite pronouns — each, every, either, neither, someone, anyone, everyone, nobody, everybody — take a singular verb (e.g., "Everyone is happy." / "Each of the students has a book.")
- **Rule 5**: "None" can take singular or plural verb depending on context, though singular is traditionally preferred (e.g., "None of them is/are guilty.")
- **Rule 6**: Subjects joined by "with," "along with," "as well as," "together with," "in addition to," "besides" — verb agrees with the first (main) subject only, not the added phrase (e.g., "The teacher, along with the students, is going on a trip.")
- **Rule 7**: Words that are plural in form but singular in meaning take singular verb — news, mathematics, physics, economics, civics, ethics, measles, mumps, athletics (e.g., "Mathematics is an interesting subject." / "The news is shocking.")
- **Rule 8**: Words that are plural in form and always plural in meaning take plural verb — scissors, trousers, spectacles, tongs, pliers, jeans (e.g., "The scissors are sharp.") — often preceded by "a pair of" which then governs a singular verb ("A pair of scissors is on the table.")
- **Rule 9**: Uncountable/mass nouns (money, furniture, information, advice, luggage, equipment, knowledge) take singular verb (e.g., "The furniture is expensive.")
- **Rule 10**: Titles of books, films, organizations, and countries, though plural in form, take singular verb (e.g., "The Arabian Nights is a fascinating book." / "The United States is a powerful nation.")
- **Rule 11**: When subject is a fraction/percentage/portion + "of" + noun, verb agrees with the noun following "of" (e.g., "Two-thirds of the work is done." / "Two-thirds of the students are present.")
- **Rule 12**: "A number of" + plural noun takes a plural verb; "The number of" + plural noun takes a singular verb (e.g., "A number of students are absent." / "The number of students is fifty.")
- **Rule 13**: In sentences beginning with "there," verb agrees with the subject that follows (e.g., "There is a book on the table." / "There are many books on the table.")
- **Rule 14**: Distance, money, time, weight as a single quantity/unit take a singular verb (e.g., "Ten kilometers is a long distance." / "Five hundred rupees is a good amount.")

## 10. Conjunctions
- Conjunction = a word that joins words, phrases, or clauses.
- **Coordinating Conjunctions** (join equal/independent clauses) — remembered by acronym **FANBOYS**: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So.
  - Example: "She is poor but honest." / "He worked hard, so he succeeded."
- **Correlative Conjunctions** (used in pairs): either...or, neither...nor, both...and, not only...but also, whether...or, as...as, such...that, no sooner...than, scarcely...when, hardly...when.
  - Example: "Not only did he apologize but also he compensated for the loss." / "No sooner had he arrived than it started raining."
- **Subordinating Conjunctions** (join a subordinate/dependent clause to a main clause):
  - Time: when, while, before, after, since, until/till, as soon as.
  - Cause/Reason: because, since, as, so that.
  - Condition: if, unless, provided that, in case.
  - Contrast/Concession: although, though, even though, whereas, while.
  - Purpose: so that, in order that.
  - Comparison: than, as...as.
  - Example: "Although it rained, we went out." / "He stayed home because he was ill."
- **Common Errors to Avoid**: Do not use "but" and "although/though" together in the same sentence (incorrect: "Although he is rich, but he is unhappy" → correct: "Although he is rich, he is unhappy"); "as soon as" should not be paired with "than"; "no sooner...than," "hardly/scarcely...when" require correct inversion in formal writing (e.g., "No sooner had the bell rung than the students left.")
- **"So...that" and "such...that"** used to show result/consequence (e.g., "He is so weak that he cannot walk." / "It was such a hot day that we stayed indoors.")

## लक्षात ठेवा / Key Points to Remember
- 12 tenses = 3 times × 4 aspects; identify keywords: "every day/usually" (Simple Present), "now/at present" (Present Continuous), "already/just/since/for" (Present Perfect), "for + duration + continuing" (Present Perfect Continuous).
- Passive voice formula shortcut: Object + correct form of "be" + V3 + by + Subject; tense of "be" must match the tense of the active sentence.
- In Indirect Speech, always check: (1) reporting verb tense, (2) pronoun change, (3) tense backshift, (4) time/place word change, (5) sentence type (statement/question/command/exclamation) to decide correct reporting verb and structure.
- "The" is used for unique things, superlatives, rivers/seas/mountain ranges, and specific/already-mentioned nouns; "a/an" for general, first-mention, singular countable nouns; zero article for proper nouns, abstract/material nouns in general sense, and plural general nouns.
- Common preposition confusions frequently asked in MPSC: arrive at/in, married to, angry with/at, different from, since/for, in/at/on for time and place.
- Subject-Verb Agreement — most-tested MPSC areas: collective nouns, "a number of" vs "the number of," "each/every," "along with" type phrases, and plural-form-singular-meaning nouns (news, mathematics).
- FANBOYS for coordinating conjunctions; correlative conjunctions always appear in fixed pairs — do not mix (e.g., "not only...but also," never "not only...and").
- Practice converting sentences across all three related areas together (tense → voice → narration) since MPSC often combines these in a single question (e.g., convert a given sentence's tense, then its voice, then report it in indirect speech).
- Always cross-check irregular verb forms (V1-V2-V3) since passive voice and perfect tenses depend on correct V3 (e.g., go-went-gone, write-wrote-written, eat-ate-eaten, buy-bought-bought, break-broke-broken).
