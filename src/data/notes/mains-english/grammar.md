# English Grammar — Tenses, Voice, Speech, Articles, Prepositions, Subject-Verb Agreement

- **Tenses**: 12 forms across Present/Past/Future × Simple/Continuous/Perfect/Perfect Continuous. Simple Present for habits/facts (V1/V5); Present Continuous = is/am/are + V-ing; Present Perfect = has/have + V3; Present Perfect Continuous = has/have been + V-ing.
- Past Simple = V2; Past Continuous = was/were + V-ing; Past Perfect = had + V3 (earlier of two past actions); Past Perfect Continuous = had been + V-ing.
- Future Simple = shall/will + V1; Future Continuous = will be + V-ing; Future Perfect = will have + V3; Future Perfect Continuous = will have been + V-ing.
- **Active to Passive**: Object becomes subject + be (matching tense) + V3 + by + subject. E.g., "He writes a letter" → "A letter is written by him."
- Passive of modal verbs: modal + be + V3 (e.g., "must be done").
- Passive of Present Perfect: has/have been + V3; Past Perfect: had been + V3; Future: will be + V3.
- No passive for intransitive verbs (sleep, arrive) or when object is absent.
- **Direct to Indirect Speech**: Reporting verb "says/say" → no tense change; "said" → tense shifts back one step (Present→Past, Past→Past Perfect).
- Pronoun changes per subject/object of reporting verb; "this→that", "these→those", "now→then", "today→that day", "tomorrow→the next day", "yesterday→the previous day", "here→there".
- Interrogative: use "asked/inquired", invert word order to statement form, add if/whether for yes-no questions; no question mark.
- Imperative: use "ordered/requested/advised" + to-infinitive (or not to + V1 for negative).
- **Articles**: "a" before consonant sound, "an" before vowel sound (an hour, a university); "the" for specific/unique nouns, superlatives, rivers, oceans, mountain ranges (the Ganga, the Himalayas); no article before proper nouns, uncountable nouns in general sense.
- **Prepositions**: fixed usage — "in" (month/year), "on" (date/day), "at" (time/point); "since" for point of time, "for" duration; "between" two, "among" more than two.
- **Subject-Verb Agreement**: Singular subject → singular verb; collective nouns (team, jury) usually take singular verb; "each", "either", "neither", "everyone" take singular verb; two subjects joined by "and" take plural verb; joined by "or/nor" verb agrees with nearer subject.
