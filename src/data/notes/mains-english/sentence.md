# English Sentence Correction & Rearrangement — MPSC Group-C Combined 2026 (Mains English)

## 1. Scope and Question Types in MPSC Exams
- Spotting Errors: A sentence is divided into 3–4 parts (underlined/segmented); candidate identifies the part containing a grammatical error, or marks "No Error."
- Sentence Improvement: A sentence has an underlined phrase; candidate picks the best replacement from options (one option is "No Improvement").
- Jumbled Sentences / Sentence Rearrangement (Para Jumbles): Parts labelled P, Q, R, S (sometimes with fixed first/last sentence) must be arranged in logical, grammatically correct order.
- Fill in the Blanks: Single or double blanks in a sentence to be filled with the most appropriate word(s) — tests vocabulary, prepositions, tenses, and idioms.
- Cloze Test (passage with multiple blanks) may also appear combined with this topic in some MPSC papers.

## 2. Subject–Verb Agreement (Concord) Rules
- A singular subject takes a singular verb; a plural subject takes a plural verb. Example: "He goes to school" / "They go to school."
- Two singular subjects joined by "and" normally take a plural verb: "Ram and Shyam are friends." Exception: if they refer to one single idea/person, use singular — "Bread and butter is my breakfast."
- Subjects joined by "each," "every," "many a," "no" before them take a singular verb even if connected by "and": "Every boy and every girl was present."
- When subjects are joined by "or," "nor," "either...or," "neither...nor," the verb agrees with the subject nearest to it: "Neither the teacher nor the students were present."
- Collective nouns (team, jury, family, committee, government, army) take a singular verb when acting as one unit, plural when members act individually: "The team is playing well" vs "The team are divided in their opinion."
- Indefinite pronouns "each," "either," "neither," "everyone," "everybody," "somebody," "anybody," "nobody," "no one" take a singular verb: "Each of the boys has a book."
- "A number of" + plural noun takes a plural verb; "The number of" + plural noun takes a singular verb: "A number of students were absent." / "The number of students is 40."
- Words joined by "with," "along with," "as well as," "together with," "in addition to," "besides," "no less than" do not change the number of the verb — verb agrees with the main subject: "The manager, along with his staff, is arriving today."
- "One of the + plural noun + who/that" clause takes a plural verb; "the only one of the + plural noun" takes singular: "He is one of the boys who play well" vs "He is the only one of the boys who plays well."
- Subjects like "mathematics," "physics," "news," "economics," "statistics" (as a discipline), "measles," "civics" are treated as singular. But "statistics" meaning data/figures is plural.
- Distance, money, weight, time when expressing a single quantity/idea take a singular verb: "Ten kilometers is a long distance." "Five thousand rupees is a huge amount."
- "There" as an introductory word: verb agrees with the real subject that follows: "There is a book on the table." / "There are many books on the table."
- Percentage/fraction + "of" + noun: verb agrees with the noun after "of": "Fifty percent of the work is done." / "Fifty percent of the students are present."
- "None" can take singular or plural verb depending on context; generally singular in formal usage: "None of us is/are perfect."
- In sentences beginning with "either...or"/"neither...nor" joining a singular and plural subject, verb agrees with the nearer subject (proximity rule).

## 3. Tense Consistency and Sequence of Tenses
- Do not mix tenses illogically within one sentence; maintain consistency unless a time-shift is clearly indicated.
- If the main clause is in the past tense, the subordinate clause must generally also be in a past form (Sequence of Tense rule): "He said that he was ill" (not "is ill").
- Exception: universal truths and habitual facts remain in present tense even if reporting verb is past: "The teacher said that the earth revolves around the sun."
- "Since" is used with Present Perfect/Past Perfect for a point of time; "for" is used for a period/duration: "I have lived here since 2015." / "I have lived here for ten years."
- Present Perfect (have/has + V3) is not used with definite past time expressions like "yesterday," "last year," "ago" — use Simple Past instead: Incorrect: "I have met him yesterday." Correct: "I met him yesterday."
- "If" clauses (Conditionals):
  - Type 1 (real/future): If + Present Simple, will + V1 — "If it rains, I will stay home."
  - Type 2 (unreal/present-future): If + Past Simple, would + V1 — "If I were rich, I would travel."
  - Type 3 (unreal past): If + Past Perfect, would have + V3 — "If I had known, I would have helped."
- "Wish"/"If only" is followed by Past Simple (present wish), Past Perfect (past wish), or "would" (for a change wanted): "I wish I knew the answer."
- "It is time," "would rather," "as if/as though" are followed by past tense forms even for present meaning: "It is time we left."
- Past Perfect (had + V3) is used for the earlier of two past actions; Simple Past for the later one: "The train had left before I reached the station."

## 4. Articles (A, An, The) — Common Errors
- "A" is used before words starting with a consonant sound; "an" before a vowel sound (based on sound, not spelling): "a university," "an hour," "a European," "an MLA."
- "The" is used for specific/particular nouns, superlatives, unique objects (the sun, the moon, the sky, the earth), rivers, seas, oceans, mountain ranges, deserts (the Ganga, the Himalayas, the Sahara, the Arabian Sea), newspapers (The Times of India), and historical periods/events.
- No article before proper nouns, names of countries (except a few like "the Netherlands," "the USA," "the UK"), languages, meals (unless specific), abstract nouns in general sense, and plural countable nouns used in a general sense.
- No article before names of single mountains, lakes (Mount Everest, Lake Victoria) — but "the" is used for mountain ranges and groups of lakes.
- Common Group-C error type: missing/wrong article, e.g., "He is honest man" (should be "an honest man"); "I saw an European" (should be "a European").

## 5. Preposition Errors (Frequently Tested)
- Correct fixed prepositions: "different from" (not "different than" in formal English), "married to," "good at," "bad at," "angry with (a person)," "angry at (a situation)," "afraid of," "proud of," "capable of," "responsible for," "similar to," "independent of," "prevent...from," "compare to" (for similarity) vs "compare with" (for detailed comparison).
- "Since" (point of time) vs "for" (duration) vs "from" (starting point, often paired with "to").
- "In" (larger area/enclosed space/months/years), "at" (specific point/time/place), "on" (days, dates, surfaces): "in Mumbai," "at the bus stop," "on Monday."
- Common confusions: "discuss about" is incorrect — correct is simply "discuss" (transitive verb, no preposition needed); similarly "emphasize on" is often flagged wrong (correct: "emphasize"), "request for" vs plain "request" (context dependent), "reply to a letter."
- "Between" is used for two things/people; "among" for more than two: "Between the two brothers" / "Among the three friends."
- Verb + preposition combos to memorize: "look after" (take care of), "look into" (investigate), "look for" (search), "look forward to" (+ V-ing), "insist on," "consist of," "deal with," "depend on," "rely on," "succeed in," "arrive at (small place)/in (large place/country)."

## 6. Common Confusable Words / Usage Errors
- "Fewer" (countable nouns) vs "less" (uncountable nouns): "fewer books," "less water."
- "Number" (countable) vs "amount" (uncountable): "a number of people," "an amount of money."
- "Many" (countable) vs "much" (uncountable).
- "Its" (possessive) vs "it's" (contraction of "it is").
- "Their" (possessive) vs "there" (place) vs "they're" (they are).
- "Effect" (noun, result) vs "affect" (verb, to influence).
- "Lay" (transitive, to place something) vs "lie" (intransitive, to recline); past tense: lay–laid–laid vs lie–lay–lain.
- "Beside" (next to) vs "besides" (in addition to).
- "Farther" (physical distance) vs "further" (figurative/additional).
- "Comprise" means "to consist of" — never write "comprised of" (a very common MPSC trap); correct: "The committee comprises 10 members."
- Double comparatives/superlatives are wrong: "more better," "most best" — incorrect; should be "better," "best."
- Double negatives are wrong: "I don't have no money" — incorrect; correct: "I don't have any money."
- Redundancy/tautology errors: "return back," "repeat again," "each and every," "free gift," "past history," "future plans" — avoid unnecessary repetition.

## 7. Word Order and Sentence Structure Errors
- Adverb placement: adverbs of frequency (always, usually, often, never, seldom, sometimes) go before the main verb but after "be" verb: "He always comes late." / "He is always late."
- "Only," "even," "also," "just" should be placed immediately before the word they modify to avoid ambiguity: "Only he can solve this" vs "He can only solve this."
- Question tags must match the main clause in tense and polarity (positive statement → negative tag, negative statement → positive tag): "You are coming, aren't you?" "She doesn't like tea, does she?"
- Parallelism/Parallel Structure: items in a list or compound structure must be grammatically parallel: Incorrect: "She likes reading, to write, and painting." Correct: "She likes reading, writing, and painting."
- Correlative conjunctions must have matching structures: "not only...but also," "either...or," "neither...nor," "both...and" — elements after each part of the pair must be parallel: "He is not only intelligent but also hardworking."
- Misplaced modifiers create ambiguity/error: "Walking down the street, the trees looked beautiful" (dangling modifier — should clarify who is walking).
- Direct/Indirect (Reported) Speech conversion errors are tested: pronoun change, tense backshift, time/place word changes (now→then, today→that day, here→there, this→that, tomorrow→the next day, yesterday→the previous day).

## 8. Sentence Rearrangement (Jumbled Sentences / Para Jumbles) — Strategy
- Step 1: Read all given parts (P, Q, R, S) and identify the topic/subject sentence — usually contains the main noun introduced generally (not "he/she/it" referring back).
- Step 2: Find the opening sentence — it should NOT begin with a pronoun (he, she, it, they) referring to something not yet introduced, nor with a conjunction like "but," "and," "so," "therefore," "however" unless context demands.
- Step 3: Identify linking words/pronouns/conjunctions (however, therefore, moreover, but, and, this, that, these, those, such, so) that connect two specific sentences — they indicate sequence.
- Step 4: Look for chronological or logical cause-effect order — events described in the order they occurred.
- Step 5: Match pronoun references — a sentence using "he/she/it/they" must follow the sentence where that noun was first mentioned.
- Step 6: Check tense flow — sentences should show a logical progression of tense (unless flashback is indicated).
- Step 7: Eliminate answer choices systematically — MPSC usually gives 4 options with different orderings; identify a definite pair (e.g., "QP always together") to narrow down options quickly.
- Step 8: For rearrangement with a fixed first sentence (S1) and last sentence (S6), only middle parts need reordering — apply same linking-word logic to middle segments.
- Common linking words to watch: "Firstly/First," "Secondly," "Then," "After that," "Finally," "In conclusion," "For example," "For instance," "In addition," "On the other hand," "Consequently," "As a result."

## 9. Fill in the Blanks — Key Focus Areas
- Vocabulary-based blanks: test synonyms/antonyms and precise word choice (contextual meaning) — e.g., "The manager was ______ (reluctant/eager) to accept the proposal."
- Preposition-based blanks: test fixed collocations, e.g., "He is good ___ mathematics" (at); "She was accused ___ theft" (of).
- Conjunction-based blanks: test logical connectors — "although," "because," "since," "unless," "whereas," "in spite of," "despite" (despite/in spite of + noun/gerund, NOT + clause with "that").
- Tense/verb-form blanks: choose correct auxiliary or participle form matching sentence context and time reference.
- Idiomatic expressions: common idioms tested include "to bell the cat" (to do something risky), "to burn the midnight oil" (to work late), "to beat around the bush" (avoid direct talk), "to add fuel to fire," "a bolt from the blue," "to break the ice," "in the nick of time," "to turn over a new leaf," "to shed crocodile tears," "an axe to grind," "to hit the nail on the head."
- Double blanks require checking that both words logically and grammatically fit together and match sentence tone (formal/informal).
- Modal verbs in blanks: can/could (ability/permission), may/might (possibility/permission), must/have to (obligation/necessity), should/ought to (advice), will/would (future/polite request) — choose based on degree of certainty/obligation implied.

## 10. Additional Grammar Points Frequently Tested by MPSC
- Active to Passive Voice conversion errors: subject-object inversion, correct form of "be" + past participle, and correct tense retention: "The teacher teaches the students" → "The students are taught by the teacher."
- Passive voice is not usually formed for intransitive verbs (e.g., "happen," "occur," "arrive," "sleep").
- Degrees of comparison errors: positive, comparative (+er/more), superlative (+est/most) — only one form of comparison should be used at a time (avoid "more taller").
- Correct use of "who" (subject, for people), "whom" (object, for people), "which" (things), "that" (things/people in restrictive clauses), "whose" (possession).
- Gerund vs Infinitive after certain verbs: some verbs are followed only by gerund (enjoy, avoid, suggest, admit, finish, consider + V-ing), others by infinitive (want, decide, plan, hope, agree + to-V), and some by either with a meaning change (stop to do vs stop doing; remember to do vs remember doing).
- Countable/uncountable noun errors: "informations," "furnitures," "advices," "equipments" are incorrect (these are uncountable, no plural "-s").
- Punctuation-related sentence correction: correct use of comma before "and" in a list of three or more items (Oxford comma optional but consistency matters), correct use of apostrophe for possession (boy's book — singular; boys' books — plural), and correct placement of quotation marks.
- Homophones commonly tested: "your/you're," "whose/who's," "principal/principle," "stationary/stationery," "compliment/complement," "council/counsel."

## लक्षात ठेवा / Key Points to Remember
- MPSC English paper for Group-C Combined typically includes questions from this topic under the "Marathi and English" language sections of Paper I/Paper II depending on post — practice standard error-spotting and para-jumble sets from previous MPSC papers.
- Always read the full sentence/paragraph first before marking an error or choosing a rearrangement — context determines correctness, not isolated words.
- "No Error" / "No Improvement" is a valid and frequently correct option — do not assume every sentence has a mistake.
- For jumbled sentences, identify at least one confirmed pair or the definite opening/closing sentence to eliminate wrong options quickly under time pressure.
- Practice identifying subject-verb agreement, tense consistency, and preposition errors first, as these are the most frequently tested error types in MPSC papers.
- Common traps: "comprised of," "discuss about," "return back," "less people," "more better," "one of the best player," "each of the students have" — memorize these as classic wrong forms.
- Time management tip: allocate roughly 30–45 seconds per error-spotting question and about 60–90 seconds per rearrangement question during the exam.
