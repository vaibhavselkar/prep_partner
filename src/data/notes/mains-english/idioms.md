# English Idioms & Phrasal Verbs — MPSC Group-C Combined 2026 (Mains English)

## Introduction & Exam Relevance
- Idioms and Phrasal Verbs form a recurring component of the Mains English paper in MPSC Group-C Combined exams, tested through "choose the correct meaning," "fill in the blank with the correct idiom," and "identify the phrasal verb" type MCQs.
- An **idiom** is a group of words whose meaning is not deducible from the individual words (figurative/non-literal meaning), e.g., "kick the bucket" = to die.
- A **phrasal verb** is a verb combined with a preposition and/or adverb (particle) that creates a meaning different from the original verb, e.g., "give up" = to stop trying/quit.
- Phrasal verbs can be **transitive** (take an object: look after someone) or **intransitive** (no object: break down).
- Phrasal verbs can be **separable** (object can go between verb and particle: "turn the light off" / "turn off the light") or **inseparable** (object must follow the whole phrase: "look after the child," not "look the child after").
- Candidates must know both the meaning and correct usage in a sentence, as questions often test contextual fit.

## Idioms Related to Body Parts
- **A cold shoulder** – deliberate unfriendliness or indifference; e.g., She gave him the cold shoulder after the argument.
- **All ears** – listening attentively; e.g., Tell me the story, I'm all ears.
- **Keep an eye on** – watch carefully; e.g., Keep an eye on the luggage.
- **Turn a blind eye** – ignore deliberately; e.g., The teacher turned a blind eye to minor mistakes.
- **Lend a hand** – to help; e.g., Can you lend a hand with these boxes?
- **Have a change of heart** – change one's opinion or feeling; e.g., He had a change of heart and forgave her.
- **Get cold feet** – become nervous/hesitant before an event; e.g., She got cold feet before her wedding.
- **Keep your chin up** – stay positive despite difficulty; e.g., Keep your chin up, things will improve.
- **A slap on the wrist** – a very mild punishment; e.g., He got just a slap on the wrist for the mistake.
- **Cost an arm and a leg** – very expensive; e.g., That car costs an arm and a leg.

## Idioms Related to Animals
- **Let the cat out of the bag** – reveal a secret accidentally; e.g., He let the cat out of the bag about the surprise party.
- **A wolf in sheep's clothing** – a dangerous person pretending to be harmless; e.g., Beware of him, he's a wolf in sheep's clothing.
- **Kill two birds with one stone** – achieve two goals with a single action; e.g., By walking to work, I kill two birds with one stone: exercise and save money.
- **Smell a rat** – suspect foul play; e.g., When the accounts didn't match, I smelled a rat.
- **A dog's life** – a miserable existence; e.g., He led a dog's life after losing his job.
- **The lion's share** – the largest portion; e.g., He took the lion's share of the profit.
- **Straight from the horse's mouth** – information from a reliable/direct source; e.g., I heard it straight from the horse's mouth.
- **When pigs fly** – something that will never happen; e.g., He'll apologize when pigs fly.
- **Hold your horses** – wait, be patient; e.g., Hold your horses, let's think this through.

## Idioms Related to Colours
- **Once in a blue moon** – very rarely; e.g., He visits once in a blue moon.
- **Green with envy** – extremely jealous; e.g., She was green with envy at her friend's success.
- **A white lie** – a harmless/small lie; e.g., He told a white lie to avoid hurting her feelings.
- **Black and white** – something completely clear, in written/printed form; e.g., Get the agreement in black and white.
- **Give the green light** – give permission to proceed; e.g., The board gave the green light for the project.
- **Red-handed** – caught in the act of doing something wrong; e.g., The thief was caught red-handed.
- **Out of the blue** – suddenly, unexpectedly; e.g., He called me out of the blue after years.
- **Golden opportunity** – an excellent chance; e.g., This job is a golden opportunity for her.

## Idioms Related to Numbers
- **At sixes and sevens** – in a state of confusion; e.g., The office was at sixes and sevens after the sudden resignation.
- **Two peas in a pod** – very similar/inseparable; e.g., The twins are two peas in a pod.
- **On cloud nine** – extremely happy; e.g., She was on cloud nine after passing the exam.
- **Back to square one** – starting over from the beginning; e.g., The plan failed, so we're back to square one.
- **In two minds** – undecided; e.g., I'm in two minds about accepting the offer.
- **Second to none** – the best; e.g., Her dedication is second to none.
- **Nine days' wonder** – something that causes excitement briefly then is forgotten; e.g., His fame was a nine days' wonder.

## Idioms Related to Time
- **In the nick of time** – just in time, at the last moment; e.g., He arrived in the nick of time to catch the train.
- **A stitch in time saves nine** – timely action prevents bigger problems.
- **Once and for all** – finally, decisively; e.g., Let's settle this once and for all.
- **Around the clock** – continuously, 24 hours; e.g., Doctors worked around the clock during the crisis.
- **In the blink of an eye** – very quickly; e.g., The accident happened in the blink of an eye.
- **Time flies** – time passes quickly; e.g., Time flies when you're having fun.
- **Beat the clock** – finish before a deadline; e.g., We managed to beat the clock and submit the report.

## Idioms Related to Money & Work
- **Break the bank** – spend more than one can afford; e.g., This vacation won't break the bank.
- **Make ends meet** – manage financially with limited money; e.g., It's hard to make ends meet on a small salary.
- **A penny for your thoughts** – asking what someone is thinking; e.g., You look worried—a penny for your thoughts?
- **Burn the midnight oil** – work late into the night; e.g., Students burn the midnight oil before exams.
- **Bite the bullet** – face a difficult situation bravely; e.g., He decided to bite the bullet and admit his mistake.
- **Hit the nail on the head** – say exactly the right thing; e.g., You hit the nail on the head with that comment.
- **A piece of cake** – something very easy; e.g., The exam was a piece of cake for her.
- **Add fuel to the fire** – make a bad situation worse; e.g., His comments only added fuel to the fire.
- **Back to the drawing board** – start planning again after failure; e.g., The proposal was rejected, so it's back to the drawing board.

## Common Phrasal Verbs — With Meanings & Examples
- **Look after** – to take care of; e.g., She looks after her younger siblings.
- **Look forward to** – to anticipate with pleasure; e.g., I look forward to meeting you.
- **Look into** – to investigate; e.g., The police will look into the matter.
- **Give up** – to quit or stop trying; e.g., He gave up smoking last year.
- **Give in** – to surrender or yield; e.g., She finally gave in to their demands.
- **Give away** – to distribute free, or reveal (a secret); e.g., His nervousness gave away his lie.
- **Put off** – to postpone; e.g., They put off the meeting till next week.
- **Put up with** – to tolerate; e.g., I can't put up with his rude behaviour.
- **Bring up** – to raise (a child) or mention a topic; e.g., She brought up an important point.
- **Bring about** – to cause something to happen; e.g., The reforms brought about major changes.
- **Call off** – to cancel; e.g., The match was called off due to rain.
- **Carry on** – to continue; e.g., Please carry on with your work.
- **Turn down** – to reject; e.g., He turned down the job offer.
- **Turn up** – to arrive, or appear unexpectedly; e.g., She turned up late for the meeting.
- **Break down** – to stop functioning, or lose emotional control; e.g., The car broke down on the highway.
- **Break out** – to start suddenly (fire, war, disease); e.g., A fire broke out in the factory.
- **Come across** – to find by chance; e.g., I came across an old photo album.
- **Come up with** – to produce/suggest (an idea); e.g., She came up with a brilliant solution.
- **Fall out** – to quarrel/have a disagreement; e.g., The two friends fell out over money.
- **Get along with** – to have a good relationship with; e.g., He gets along well with his colleagues.
- **Get over** – to recover from; e.g., It took her weeks to get over the illness.
- **Go through** – to experience, or examine carefully; e.g., He is going through a difficult time.
- **Hold on** – to wait; e.g., Hold on a minute, I'll be right there.
- **Keep up with** – to stay at the same pace/level; e.g., She struggled to keep up with the class.
- **Make up** – to invent (a story), or reconcile after a quarrel; e.g., They made up after the fight.
- **Point out** – to indicate/draw attention to; e.g., The teacher pointed out the errors.
- **Run out of** – to have no more of something; e.g., We ran out of sugar.
- **Set up** – to establish/arrange; e.g., They set up a new business.
- **Take after** – to resemble (a family member); e.g., She takes after her mother.
- **Take off** – (aircraft) to leave the ground, or become successful; e.g., The plane took off on time.
- **Work out** – to exercise, or to solve/succeed; e.g., The plan worked out well in the end.
- **Pull off** – to succeed in doing something difficult; e.g., They pulled off a stunning victory.
- **Pass away** – to die (polite expression); e.g., Her grandfather passed away last year.
- **Show off** – to display proudly/boastfully; e.g., He loves to show off his new car.
- **Stand for** – to represent/symbolize, or to tolerate; e.g., UN stands for United Nations.

- **Red tape** – excessive bureaucratic rules/procedures, often used in reference to government administration (relevant to MPSC candidates dealing with government processes).
- **A tug of war** – a struggle for power/control between two parties; commonly used in political/administrative reporting.
- **Iron out differences** – resolve disagreements through discussion; common in political/diplomatic news.
- **On the same page** – in agreement; used in administrative/team contexts.
- **Rock the boat** – disturb a stable situation; often seen in governance-related editorials.
- **A drop in the ocean** – a very small amount compared to what is needed; used in budget/welfare-scheme discussions.
- **Fall through the cracks** – be overlooked, especially in systems/schemes (relevant to welfare/policy discussions).

## Question Format & Exam Strategy Tips
- Questions typically present a sentence and ask candidates to select the option that best replaces/explains the underlined idiom or phrasal verb.
- Some questions give the idiom's meaning and ask to identify the correct idiom.
- Fill-in-the-blank format questions require selecting the correct particle/preposition to complete a phrasal verb (e.g., "The meeting was called ___ due to bad weather" → off).
- Candidates should learn idioms in context (sentences) rather than isolated word lists to correctly judge usage and register (formal/informal).
- Watch out for near-synonym traps: e.g., "look into" (investigate) vs. "look after" (take care) vs. "look forward to" (anticipate) – all differ only by the added particle.
- Standard reference books used for MPSC English preparation include Wren & Martin's "High School English Grammar and Composition" and various MPSC-specific English guides that list idioms and phrasal verbs categorically.

## लक्षात ठेवा / Key Points to Remember
- Idiom = fixed figurative expression (meaning ≠ sum of individual words); Phrasal verb = verb + particle(s) forming new meaning.
- Always learn idioms/phrasal verbs with example sentences to understand correct contextual usage.
- Group idioms by theme (body parts, animals, colours, numbers, time, money) for easier memorization.
- Note separable vs. inseparable phrasal verbs, as this affects sentence structure in fill-in-the-blank questions.
- Pay special attention to near-identical phrasal verbs with different particles (look after/look into/look forward to; give up/give in/give away).
- Practice previous years' MPSC English question papers to identify frequently repeated idioms and phrasal verbs.
- Idioms related to governance/bureaucracy (red tape, cut the red tape, iron out differences) are especially relevant given the administrative nature of MPSC Group-C posts.
