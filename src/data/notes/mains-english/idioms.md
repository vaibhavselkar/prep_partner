# English Idioms & Phrasal Verbs

- Idiom = fixed phrase whose meaning differs from literal words (e.g., "kick the bucket" = to die); learn meaning, not word-by-word translation.
- Phrasal verb = verb + particle (preposition/adverb) forming new meaning (e.g., "give up" = quit, "look after" = take care of).
- Transitive phrasal verbs need an object ("put off the meeting"); intransitive don't ("break down").
- Separable phrasal verbs: object can go between or after verb-particle ("turn off the light" / "turn the light off"); with pronoun object, must separate ("turn it off", not "turn off it").
- Inseparable phrasal verbs: object always after ("look after the child", not "look the child after").
- Common exam idioms: "once in a blue moon" (rarely), "spill the beans" (reveal secret), "beat around the bush" (avoid topic), "hit the nail on the head" (exactly right), "add fuel to fire" (worsen situation).
- High-frequency phrasal verbs: bring up (raise topic/rear), call off (cancel), carry out (execute), come across (find by chance), do away with (abolish), get along with (have good relation), look forward to (anticipate happily), put up with (tolerate), run out of (exhaust supply), take after (resemble).
- MPSC favors idioms with body parts/animals/colors: "a red herring" (misleading clue), "let the cat out of the bag" (reveal), "cost an arm and a leg" (very expensive), "under the weather" (unwell).
- Practice matching-type and fill-in-blank formats; MPSC often gives meaning and asks correct idiom, or vice versa.
- Memorize idiom meaning + one sample sentence together for retention.
- Avoid literal translation of Marathi idioms into English in answers; use standard English equivalents.
- Revise via categorized lists (weather, animals, body parts, colors, food) for faster recall.
- Practice previous MPSC papers' idiom/phrasal verb questions for repeated patterns.
