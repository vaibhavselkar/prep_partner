# Reading Comprehension / वाचन समझ

• **Main Idea vs. Details**: Distinguish between the central theme and supporting examples. Main idea is usually stated in the first/last paragraph or can be inferred from the overall passage.

• **Inference vs. Fact**: Never confuse what is explicitly stated with what you infer. Mark "NOT directly stated" inferences with caution—use only direct textual clues.

• **Tone & Purpose**: Identify author's intent (inform, persuade, criticize, entertain). Tone keywords—critical, sarcastic, formal, sympathetic—reveal bias and intent.

• **Vocabulary in Context**: Don't rely on dictionary meanings alone. Use surrounding words and sentence structure to deduce unfamiliar word meanings.

• **Pronoun Reference**: Always trace pronouns (he, she, it, they) to their antecedents. Misidentifying reference = wrong answer.

• **Explicit vs. Implicit**: Explicit = directly stated; Implicit = suggested but not stated. Test questions heavily weight implicit understanding.

• **Sequencing & Cause-Effect**: Track logical flow—why events happen, what precedes/follows. This reveals author's argument structure.

• **Fact vs. Opinion**: Facts are verifiable; opinions reflect beliefs. Identify opinion markers: "I think," "should," "better," adjectives.

• **Supporting Evidence**: Answer choices often use correct words but wrong context. Verify that the supporting detail actually backs the claim.

• **Title Selection**: The best title captures the main idea, not minor details. Eliminate overly broad or overly narrow options.

• **Passage Strategy**: Read questions first, then skim passage for relevant sections. Don't read the entire passage word-by-word in exams.

• **Contradiction Detection**: If a conclusion contradicts the passage, eliminate it immediately.

• **Time Management**: Allocate ~2-3 minutes per passage (typically 300-400 words) and ~1 minute per question.
